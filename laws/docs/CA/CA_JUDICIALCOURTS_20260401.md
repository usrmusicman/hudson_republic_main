# Judicial Courts Act

---

## Definitions
All capitalized terms used in this Act shall be interpreted in accordance with their definitions in the referenced instruments below.

### Verdicts
- All Supreme Court decisions are rendered as **passed** or **failed**.  
- All lower court decisions are rendered as **guilty** or **not-guilty**.

---

## Preamble
The judicial power of the Hudson Republic is vested in one unified, four-tier court system designed for efficiency, impartiality, accessibility, and finality. This Act establishes the structure, jurisdiction, procedures, and safeguards of the courts to ensure justice is swift, transparent, and faithful to the immutable rights of the Individual as enshrined in **[The Individual (Sovereign) Act](./CA_THEINDIVIDUAL_20260401.md)**. All proceedings shall uphold the presumption of innocence in criminal matters, the right to a fair hearing, and the prohibition against double jeopardy except as expressly provided. This Act operates in coordination with the **[Elections Act](./CA_ELECTIONS_20260401.md)**, the **[Elected Officials and Integrity Act](./CA_ELECTEDOFFICIALSINTEGRITY_20260401.md)**, and the **[Legislative Framework Act](./CA_LEGISLATIVEFRAMEWORK_20260401.md)**.

---

## Section 1 — Court Hierarchy and Jurisdiction
The courts are organized in ascending order of authority:

### 1.1 County Court
The primary trial court with original jurisdiction over most civil, criminal, and ordinary matters within a single Riding or County. It serves as the court of first instance for the majority of disputes.

### 1.2 Riding Court
The intermediate appellate court for County Court decisions. It holds original jurisdiction over Riding-specific administrative, electoral, or local disputes.

### 1.3 Superior Court
The highest appellate court for most matters below the Supreme Court. It hears appeals from Riding Courts and has original jurisdiction over significant regional or multi-Riding civil and criminal cases.

### 1.4 Supreme Court
The final appellate court and guardian of constitutional review. Composed of **twelve (12) justices**. The Supreme Court may identify constitutional issues and trigger Legislative Challenges (LCs) to be reviewed by the Senate, but it does not render final verdicts on the constitutionality of legislation. Final constitutional authority rests with the Senate.

---

## Section 2 — Supreme Court Procedures
**2.1 Composition**: Twelve justices serve on the Supreme Court.  
**2.2 Quorum**: A minimum of seven (7) justices is required to hear a case.  
**2.3 Role in Constitutional Review**: When the Supreme Court identifies a potential constitutional defect in legislation or executive action, it may draft and refer a Legislative Challenge (LC) to the Senate for review. The Senate holds final authority on all constitutional questions.

---

## Section 3 — Appeals Process
Appeals follow a three-strike model unless elevated directly to the Supreme Court for exceptional constitutional matters. No case may exceed three levels of appeal except in extraordinary circumstances certified by the Supreme Court.

---

## Section 4 — Legislative Challenges and Constitutional Review
**4.1 Legislative Challenges (LCs)**: Any LC filed with the Senate triggers a full review for constitutional consistency.  
- **Constitutional Amendments**: Require a unanimous verdict (12/12) on three readings.  
- **Regular Legislation**: Require a clear verdict (7/12).  
- **Enforcement**: Validated LCs require the Executive Office to decommission or amend the legislation within **twenty-four (24) hours**.

**4.2 Referral from Supreme Court**:
- Allegations of constitutional violations may originate at the Superior Court for unambiguous legislation.  
- Ambiguous or nationally significant conflicts are referred by the Supreme Court to the Senate via a Legislative Challenge.  
- The Supreme Court may draft recommended LCs for amendments or budget adjustments, but the Senate retains final decision-making authority.

---

## Section 5 — No Specialized Courts
**5.1** The Republic maintains **no** separate mental-health, family, or administrative tribunals.  
**5.2** All matters are heard within the general court hierarchy.  
**5.3** Judges and juries are assigned based on demonstrated expertise relevant to the specific case.
